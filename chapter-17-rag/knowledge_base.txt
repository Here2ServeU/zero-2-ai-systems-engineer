Know Your Customer (KYC) Requirements. Before onboarding any customer, a financial institution must verify the customer's identity using reliable, independent source documents. For an individual, the required documents are a government-issued photo identification (such as a passport or driver's license), proof of current residential address dated within the last 90 days (such as a utility bill or bank statement), and a valid taxpayer identification number. For a business customer, the institution must additionally collect certificate of incorporation, beneficial ownership information for any party owning 25 percent or more, and the identities of authorized signatories. KYC records must be retained for a minimum of five years after the account is closed.

Customer Due Diligence and Risk Rating. Every customer is assigned a risk rating of low, medium, or high at onboarding based on geography, product, channel, and expected transaction behavior. High-risk customers, including politically exposed persons (PEPs) and customers in high-risk jurisdictions, require Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD): senior management approval to open the account, additional documentation on source of funds and source of wealth, and more frequent ongoing review. Risk ratings must be reviewed at least annually and whenever a triggering event, such as a large unexpected transaction, occurs.

Anti-Money-Laundering (AML) Reporting. The institution must file a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) with the relevant financial intelligence unit whenever it knows, suspects, or has reason to suspect that a transaction involves funds derived from illegal activity, is designed to evade reporting requirements, or has no apparent lawful purpose. A SAR must be filed within 30 calendar days of detecting the suspicious activity. The institution must not disclose to the customer that a SAR has been filed ("no tipping off"). SAR records and supporting documentation must be retained for five years.

Currency Transaction Reporting. Any cash transaction, or series of related cash transactions in a single business day, that exceeds 10,000 dollars must be reported via a Currency Transaction Report (CTR) within 15 days. Structuring — deliberately breaking a large transaction into smaller amounts to avoid the reporting threshold — is itself a reportable suspicious activity.

Transaction Monitoring Standards. All transactions are screened in real time against the institution's risk rules and in batch against behavioral models. Monitoring must detect velocity anomalies (an unusual number of transactions per account in a short window), amount anomalies (transactions that deviate from the customer's established baseline), geographic anomalies (transactions originating far from the customer's home location), and known fraud typologies. Each alert is assigned to an analyst, investigated, and dispositioned as a false positive or escalated for SAR review. Alert disposition reasoning must be documented and auditable.

Sanctions and Watchlist Screening. Every customer and every counterparty must be screened at onboarding and on an ongoing basis against applicable sanctions lists (such as OFAC), politically exposed persons lists, and adverse media. A confirmed match must block the transaction and trigger immediate escalation to the compliance officer. Screening lists must be refreshed at least daily.

Recordkeeping and Auditability. For every automated decision the platform makes — approving, declining, or flagging a transaction — the institution must be able to reproduce, on request from a regulator, the exact model version that ran, the input features, the output score, the decision threshold, and the date and time. Records of decisions, model versions, and training datasets must be retained for a minimum of five years and protected against tampering.

Model Governance for Fraud Detection. Any machine learning model used in a fraud or AML decision must be validated before deployment and revalidated at least annually. Validation must document the training data lineage, the performance metrics (including recall on known fraud cases), the demographic fairness analysis, and the explainability method used to justify individual decisions. A model whose recall on the validation set falls below the institution's regulatory minimum may not be promoted to production.
